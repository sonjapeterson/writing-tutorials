Step One

Overall Structure: Write a Reverse Outline

It's easy to get bogged down in details when you're revising, so let's start by taking a look at your paper as a whole. Doing a reverse outline will help you see if the structure of your paper really supports your argument, and make sure there aren't any missing steps.

To Do:

Re-read your paper. For each body paragraph, write down one sentence about what it's saying and how it moves your argument forward. Then, consider the questions to the right.

Strategies:

  • Consider sequence: Does the paragraphs go from making simpler claims to making more complex ones, with logical links from one to the next? If not, consider how you might rearrange them into a sequence that makes more sense.
  • Look for gaps: Are there any "holes" in your argument—places where you might need to provide an additional paragraph between two others that don't seem to relate logically?
  • Streamline: Or, are there any redundancies—paragraphs that don't contribute to your argument, or can't be distinguished from other paragraphs?
  • Focus: Finally, are there any paragraphs for which you can't summarize the topic and what it contributes to its argument in a sentence or two? Those paragraphs might have too many ideas in them, and need to broken down into multiple paragraphs, or streamlined into a paragraph with one main claim.
